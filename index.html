1. What is elasticity?

Elasticity = how much one thing moves when you change another.
Like a rubber band. Pull a little → stretches a lot = elastic. Pull a lot → barely moves = inelastic.

We care: price or income changes → do people buy way more/less (elastic) or almost the same (inelastic)?

2. Price elasticity of demand

1Price goes up or down → people buy more or less. Price elasticity = how much that “more or less” is.

The formula (from your slides):

In 3 words: Response to price.
Example
Ice cream gets expensive → lots of people buy less. So demand is elastic (big response).

3. Elastic vs inelastic

Elastic (E > 1): Price changes a little → quantity changes a LOT. (e.g. fancy chocolates.)
Inelastic (E < 1): Price changes a LOT → quantity changes a little. (e.g. medicine you need every day.)

Perfectly inelastic (E = 0)

Price goes up or down → people buy the same amount. Demand curve is vertical.

Quantity does not change when price changes

Example: Insulin for a diabetic — they need the same amount no matter the price.

Perfectly elastic (E = ∞)

Tiny price change → people go from buying zero to all they can. Demand curve is flat.

At any price above this level, quantity demanded is zero

Example: A small farmer selling wheat — raise price a bit and buyers switch to others.

4. Total revenue (what sellers earn)

2Total revenue = Price × Quantity. So TR = P × Q.

When you change price, does total revenue go up or down? It depends whether demand is elastic or inelastic.

Total revenue = P × Q

Revenue is the area (price × quantity)

Inelastic demand: price UP → revenue UP

People still buy almost the same, so higher price means more money for sellers.

Price $1 → $3: revenue $100 → $240

Inelastic: quantity falls only a little when price rises, so total revenue increases.

Elastic demand: price UP → revenue DOWN

People buy much less, so higher price means less money for sellers.

Price $4 → $5: revenue $200 → $100

Elastic: quantity falls a lot when price rises, so total revenue decreases.

Remember: Elastic → price up = revenue down. Inelastic → price up = revenue up.

Elastic (E > 1)Total revenue ↓Total revenue ↑
Unit elastic (E = 1)UnchangedUnchanged
Inelastic (E < 1)Total revenue ↑Total revenue ↓

What makes demand elastic or inelastic?

  • Substitutes: Lots of similar stuff (e.g. other ice cream) → more elastic.
  • Big part of budget: Rent, food → you react more → more elastic.
  • Luxury vs need: Luxury = elastic. Need (medicine, rice) = inelastic.

5. Income elasticity

3When your income goes up or down, do you buy more or less of this thing?
Positive = normal good (income up → buy more). Negative = inferior good (income up → buy less, you switch to better stuff).
Example
Normal: more money → more fruits, gym. Inferior: more money → less instant noodles, more real food.

6. Cross elasticity

4When the price of another good changes, how much does demand for this good change?
From your lecture (substitutes)

Coke & Pepsi: Coke price up → people buy more Pepsi. Same the other way. = Substitutes (positive cross elasticity).

Cross elasticityMeaningExample

Complements
Petrol price up → people buy fewer cars (or smaller cars). Price of one up → demand for the other down = complements.

7. Supply elasticity

5When price changes, how much do sellers change how much they make/sell?
Elastic supply: Price up a little → they make a lot more. Inelastic: Price up a lot → they can’t make much more.
Example
Elastic: t-shirts (factories can make more fast). Inelastic: rare paintings (can’t make more originals).

Recap (read this before the test)

  • Elasticity = how much one thing responds to another.
  • Price elasticity: E > 1 = elastic (big response), E < 1 = inelastic (small response).
  • TR = P × Q. Elastic: P up → TR down. Inelastic: P up → TR up.
  • Income: positive = normal good, negative = inferior good.
  • Cross: positive = substitutes, negative = complements.
  • Supply elasticity = how much sellers change how much they make when price changes.
